Email: goeun678@gmail.com

Biography

I am a research engineer specializing in computer vision, 3D perception, and AI systems for autonomous robotics.
My work focuses on building reliable perception algorithms that operate in real-world environments, particularly where safety and robustness are critical.

I received my M.S. from Seoul National University, where my research focused on LiDAR-based 3D object detection in a indoor crowded environment .
I completed my B.S. at Gachon University, majoring in medical image processing and AI-based medical diagnostic systems.

My research interests lie in bridging theoretical deep learning methods with deployable AI systems, including:

  • 3D Object Detection with LiDAR and Multi-modal Sensors
  • Vision-based Perception for Autonomous Robots
  • Foundation Models for Real-world AI Systems
  • Reliable AI through Data-centric Understanding and Optimization

Research Vision

My goal is to design AI perception systems that are not only accurate in benchmarks but also trustworthy in real operational environments such as hospitals, where robots must safely interact with humans, equipment, and dynamic surroundings.

I am particularly interested in integrating:

  • geometric understanding from 3D sensing
  • representation learning from deep neural networks
  • system-level optimization for real-time deployment

    Can Quantum-Mechanical Description of Physical Reality Be Considered Complete?
    A. Einstein*†, B. Podolsky*, and N. Rosen*
    Phys. Rev., New Jersey. More Information can be found here , May 1935
    Abs DOI HTML PDF Video

    In a complete theory there is an element corresponding to each element of reality. A sufficient condition for the reality of a physical quantity is the possibility of predicting it with certainty, without disturbing the system. In quantum mechanics in the case of two physical quantities described by non-commuting operators, the knowledge of one precludes the knowledge of the other. Then either (1) the description of reality given by the wave function in quantum mechanics is not complete or (2) these two quantities cannot have simultaneous reality. Consideration of the problem of making predictions concerning a system on the basis of measurements made on another system that had previously interacted with it leads to the result that if (1) is false then (2) is also false. One is thus led to conclude that the description of reality as given by a wave function is not complete.
